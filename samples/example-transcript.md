# Ship the ugly version first

*Talk transcript — approximately 6 minutes.*

---

I want to start with a confession. For about three years, I had a side project I told everyone I was "working on." I wasn't working on it. I was thinking about it. I had a Notion doc with 74 pages. I had a domain name. I had a Figma file with six different versions of the logo. What I did not have was a single user.

In those same three years, a friend of mine built something roughly similar. Hers was ugly. The buttons didn't line up. The name was bad. She launched it in a weekend and posted it on a forum. By the time I was picking my seventh logo variant, she had 400 users telling her what to fix.

Guess who built the better product? It wasn't me.

---

The lesson I pulled out of that — and I've watched this happen in probably a hundred other projects since, mine and other people's — is that shipping the ugly version first isn't a shortcut. It isn't a "move fast and break things" slogan. It's the actual work.

Here's why.

When you don't ship, you don't get information. You get opinions — your own and whoever you show the thing to in private. Opinions are nearly free and nearly worthless. A friend telling you they'd "probably use that" is not a signal. A stranger who clicks one button and leaves without clicking the next one — that's a signal. That's a thing you can work with.

The whole reason to ship an ugly version is not because ugliness is a virtue. It's because you literally cannot see what you've built until someone who doesn't care about your feelings tries to use it. Your brain fills in the gaps. It finishes the half-finished sentences. It forgives the confusing button because you know what the button does. A first-time user doesn't have any of that charity.

So you have to ship to see the thing. And most people spend months trying to build a thing they can't see.

---

Now — I know what the objection is, because I've made this objection myself about eighty times. "If I ship something bad, people will see that it's bad and then they won't come back. I only get one first impression."

Two things about this.

First, the audience for your ugly V1 is not "people." It is approximately twelve humans, most of whom you will hand the link to yourself. You are not burning your brand. Your brand, at this stage, does not exist. Nobody is watching. That's the whole gift of being early.

Second, and this is the one I had to learn the hard way: the version you're protecting your reputation for — the polished one, the one you're going to ship when it's ready — does not exist. It has never existed. It will not exist. The thing you eventually ship, if you ship at all, will be version 14, and it will look nothing like the polished one in your head. The polished version is a fantasy that is actively stopping you from making the real one. You're not trading an ugly launch for a polished launch. You're trading an ugly launch for no launch.

---

Okay. So what does "ship the ugly version" actually mean, practically, if you're sitting on something right now?

It means: pick the smallest surface of the thing that can be tested by a stranger. Not the whole product. Not even a quarter of it. The smallest slice where someone can complete one action end to end and tell you whether that action was worth doing.

For my friend, with her ugly launch — the slice was "sign up, enter one piece of information, get one piece of output." That was it. No profile pages. No settings. No dashboard. One input, one output, a form that worked.

The thing people miss is that the ugly version is not a worse version of the full thing. It's a different thing entirely. It's a thing designed to answer one question: does anybody want this? You build the whole product to deliver value. You build the ugly version to extract information. Those are different jobs, and they require different designs.

If you try to answer "does anybody want this" by building a full product, you will spend two years finding out the answer is no. If you answer it with an ugly version, you find out in two weeks. That's not a small difference. That's the entire game.

---

I'll wrap with the thing I wish I'd heard when I was on page 74 of that Notion doc.

Perfectionism is not a higher standard. It's a defense mechanism. It's a very sophisticated way of not having to find out if your idea is any good. Because as long as you're polishing, you can still believe. The moment you ship, belief gets replaced with evidence, and evidence is scarier than belief, even when the evidence is good.

So if you're sitting on something — and I suspect a bunch of you are, because most people are — the move is not to make it better before you ship it. The move is to ship a worse version than you're comfortable with, today or this week, to twelve people whose reactions you can read.

You'll find out more in those twelve reactions than in the next three months of polishing. And the thing you're so afraid will damage your reputation? Nobody will remember it. Nobody will remember the ugly version. They'll remember the one you eventually built because you finally had real information to build it with.

That's the thing I wish someone had just shaken me by the shoulders and told me. Ship the ugly one. The rest of the work starts the day after.
