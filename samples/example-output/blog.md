# Ship the Ugly Version First

For three years, I had a side project I told everyone I was "working on." I had a 74-page Notion doc, a domain name, and six logo variants in Figma. What I didn't have was a single user.

In those same three years, a friend built something roughly similar. The buttons didn't line up. The name was forgettable. She launched it in a weekend and posted it on a forum. By the time I was choosing between logo options five and six, she had 400 users telling her exactly what to fix.

Guess which product got better. It wasn't mine.

## You Can't See What You've Built Until a Stranger Uses It

The case for shipping early is usually framed as speed — move fast, iterate, don't overthink it. That framing misses the actual reason.

When you don't ship, you don't get information. You get opinions — mostly your own, and whoever you've shown it to privately. A friend saying they'd "probably use that" is not a signal. A stranger who clicks one button and leaves without clicking the next one — that's a signal you can do something with.

Your brain fills in every gap in your own product. It finishes the half-finished sentences. It forgives the confusing button because you know what the button does. A first-time user doesn't have that charity. So you have to ship to see the thing clearly. And most people spend months trying to build something they fundamentally cannot see yet.

## The Polished Version Doesn't Exist

I know the objection, because I've made it myself about eighty times. "If I ship something bad, people will see that it's bad. I only get one first impression."

Two things about this.

First, the audience for a V1 is not "people." It's approximately twelve humans, most of whom you hand the link to directly. You are not burning a reputation. At this stage, your reputation in this space doesn't exist. Nobody is watching. That's the gift of being early.

Second — and this one took me a long time to accept — the polished version you're protecting your reputation for doesn't exist. It has never existed. Whatever you eventually ship, if you ship at all, will be version 14, and it will look nothing like the clean design in your head. The polished version is a fantasy that's actively blocking the real one. You're not trading an ugly launch for a better launch. You're trading an ugly launch for no launch.

Perfectionism is a defense mechanism. It's a sophisticated way of not having to find out whether your idea is any good. As long as you're polishing, you can still believe. The moment you ship, belief gets replaced with evidence, and evidence is scarier than belief — even when the evidence turns out to be good.

## What "Ugly Version" Actually Means

If you're sitting on something right now, here's what this looks like in practice: pick the smallest surface of the thing that can be tested by a stranger. Not the whole product. Not even a quarter of it. The smallest slice where someone can complete one action end-to-end and tell you whether that action was worth doing.

For my friend's ugly launch, the slice was: sign up, enter one piece of information, get one piece of output. No profile pages, no settings, no dashboard. One input, one output, a form that worked.

What people miss is that the ugly version isn't a worse version of the full product. It's a different thing entirely — something designed to answer one specific question: does anybody want this? You build the full product to deliver value. You build the ugly version to extract information. Those are different jobs, and they require different designs.

If you try to answer "does anybody want this" by building the full product, you might spend two years finding out the answer is no. If you answer it with the ugly version, you find out in two weeks. That difference is the entire game.

The twelve reactions you get from an ugly, early launch will tell you more than the next three months of polishing. And the version you were so afraid would damage your reputation? Nobody will remember it. They'll remember the one you eventually built, because you finally had real information to build it with.

The rest of the work starts the day after you ship.
