For three years I had a "side project." 74-page Notion doc. A domain. Six logo variants. Zero users.

A friend built something similar. Ugly buttons. Bad name. Launched in a weekend on a forum.

By the time I was picking logo #7, she had 400 users telling her what to fix.

---

The thing I kept telling myself: "I just need it to be a little more polished before I show people."

That sentence is a trap. It sounds like a standard. It's actually a defense mechanism.

---

You cannot see what you built until someone who doesn't care about your feelings tries to use it.

Your brain fills in the gaps. It forgives the confusing button because you know what it does.

A stranger has none of that charity. That's the whole point.

---

The objection I made to myself about 80 times: "I only get one first impression. If I ship something bad, they'll think it's bad."

Your V1 audience is roughly 12 humans, most of whom you'll hand the link to yourself.

Your brand does not exist yet. Nobody is watching.

---

Second thing, and this one hit harder.

The polished version you're protecting your reputation for doesn't exist. It has never existed.

What you eventually ship will be version 14. It will look nothing like the thing in your head.

You're not trading an ugly launch for a clean one. You're trading it for no launch.

---

So what does "ship ugly" actually mean if you're sitting on something right now?

Pick the smallest slice a stranger can complete end to end. One input, one output. No settings, no profile pages, no dashboard.

Not a worse version of the full product. A different thing entirely — designed to answer one question: does anyone want this?

---

Build the full product to deliver value. Build the ugly version to extract information. Those are different jobs.

Answer "does anyone want this" by building the full product: two years to find out the answer is no.

Answer it with an ugly version: two weeks.

---

The ugly version won't damage your reputation. People don't remember your v1. They only ever know you from the thing that worked.

Ship it. That thing gets built next.
